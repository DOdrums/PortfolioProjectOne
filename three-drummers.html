Three Drummers

To get a good feel for what big band drumming should sound like, we'll have a listen to three drummers. There are a good many jazz and big band drummers that could play a roll in the development of your style, but these three drummers personally had a big impact on how I approach big band playing. It is encouraged to listen to many different big bands and drummers, to get a broad profile of the big band drummer.

Mel Lewis

We'll start off this section with Mel Lewis, a drummer with a very particular sound. Known for his work with the 'Mel Lewis/Thad Jones Orchestra', he has an amazing track record, playing with many of the greats in jazz music.

Mel Lewis was unique in his big band playing, heaving a very relaxed sound and playing almost exclusively at softer volumes. In the book 'The View From The Back Of The Band' (a biography of Mel Lewis), he is quoted having a conversation with Stan Kenton:

"When I joined Kenton, that very first night, he came over and yelled, 'Hey Mel, can you play louder?' And I said, 'No'. He said, 'Okay, I just thought I'd ask'."

Mel said he never felt it was necessary to play loud, but don't let that fool you, his playing was very dynamic. He knew how to create tension and play with energy, without overwelming the band. In his own big band, the Mel Lewis/Thad Jones Orchestra, this very much defined their sound.

In keeping with his relaxed style, Mel played behind the beat. Compared to the examples we listened to on the previous page, from the Count Basie big band, there is a noticable difference in Mel's drive, albeit only slightly. His time keeping lays a bit more behind the beat than that of Sonny Payne, but he is still able to keep a clear drive.

Another thing that stands out in Mel's playing is his tendency to play melodically. This means, that not only does he play a melodic fill to lead into the accent, he will also play along with the accent, mimicking the time and feel of the melody played by the horns.

A short note on his drum set: Mel played exclusively on Gretch drums, which had a warm and rich sound. His cymbals were Istanbuls, with a 21 inch and 19 inch crash/ride and the famous swish knocker (a thin china cymbal with rivets in the edges). His cymbals had a dark sound with plenty of wash, a sound signature loved by many jazz drummers.

Butch Miles

Next on the list is Butch Miles. This drummer could arguably be Mel Lewis' counterpart. A drummer known for his loud and explosive playing, Butch Miles is a very different sounding drummer from Mel Lewis.

Where Mel Lewis uses flow as his weapon of choice, for Butch Miles it would be contrast. He loves playing very loud and sudden fills, that seem to come out of nowhere. The accent that follows is played loudly and clearly, often on snare drum.

Butch tends to break up his time a lot as well, playing loud crash cymbals and base drums along with accents or even in between accents. Compare this to Mel Lewis who loves to play his fills in a very flowy feeling, often incorparating his swinging ride in his fills. Butch Miles' tendency to use his base drum to seperate loud accents creates very energetic sections which seem to have explosions of energy coming out of his drum kit and the band.

Butch's drum set was usually a Ludwig. It sounds a bit more open and less warm than Mel Lewis' Gretsch, but is still considered a warm soundig kit overall. Also Miles' cymbals (Zildjians) are slightly more 'pingy' and have less wash than the Istanbuls used by Lewis. The biggest difference in sound however, is how Miles tunes his snare drum. It's a higher pitched snare drum, which makes it easier to cut through the band.
