Reading and Playing

A big part of playing big band music well, comes down to being a good reader. Most big band drummers are exceptional readers. However, reading a drum sheet isn't simply playing the notes that are written down, it's all about interpreting those notes. Since this resource is meant for experienced drummers and conductors, it is expected you already know the basics of how to read sheet music.

*Use your phone in landscape orientation for better readability of the sheet music

The piece pictured below is a snippet of the song 'Basie - Straight Ahead' as played by the 'Count Basie Orchestra'. It's a medium-up swing tune, with very loud tutti sections.

In any swing piece, the drummer will play a basic swing pattern on ride cymbal and hi-hat on beat 2 and 4. Sometimes the bass drum is “feathered”, meaning it will play very softly on each beat, to create more momentum in the groove. Unless told otherwise, the drummer will continue doing this.

In this piece, you can see a few rhythms written out, which will be the moment for the drummer to break up his swing pattern and start filling and playing along with the horn sections. Have a look at the circled section at the end of the piece, and see how those notes differ from the rest. Both the notes in and above the staff represent rhythms that are played by the horns, or the rhythm section. However, the rhythms written above the staff will usually be played with less emphasis than the rhythms inside the staff. It is your job as a big band drummer to interpret how best to play the notes, in order to make the big band sound good.

Look at the piece below, which is a snippet of Count Basie's 'All Of Me'. A famous performance, from the album 'Live At The Sands Hotel'.

Sonny Payne plays the drums on this entire album, and he perfectly interprets the notes on the paper. Notice that in this snippet, all the notes are written above the staff.

Listen to the audio example below and note how Payne mostly continues his time playing and only accentuates the accents with his base drum, preceded by short fills on his snare drum (probably played with his left hand, since the time-keeping continues).

Besides writing horn notes above the staff, it's also common to write notes inside the staff. This indicates you should play the notes with more emphasis. Have a look at the sheet below, to see what that looks like.

As you can see, not only are the notes written inside the staff, it also reads 'Loud Orch.' above the staff, which tells you it will be a particularly loud section. So, you should play this part loudly and with emphasis and you'll probably want to play a fill leading up to the horn accents.

It's important to note that the quality and way of writing drum sheets can be entirely dependent on who the arranger is. As you can see, I scribbled some notes into the sheet, for clarification. This is standard practice, as the arranger usually choses not to overcrowd the sheet, so the drummer can make their own decisions on how to play the piece. During a rehearsal, you should find yourself writing often.

Some of the notes in this second section could have been written inside the staff, to make it more clear they should be played with force. Getting skilled at deciphering big band drum sheets is a matter of experience and the more you'll do it, the more you'll find yourself making correct guesses as to how a piece should be played.

Best Practices

Let's establish a few “rules” (best practices), to create some guidelines on how to play accents and how to lead the band into an accent. For this, we'll use Basie's 'Get Me To The Church On Time', again from the 'Live At The Sands Hotel' album.

How a drummer plays accents and fills in big band music is quite personal, however, you can find similarities between many different drummers. Let's use these similarities as a sort of “rule book” for playing big band music. Remember that exceptions are many and as a drummer, you should always develop your own style. These rules are meant as a tool and guide, not to be followed religiously.

Take a look at the sheet below, listen to the music and notice the ways in which the accents and fills are being played. Now read through the 'Horn Accents' and 'Fills' bullet points and give the track another listen.

Horn Accents

In general:

  • Use of base drum + crash/ride cymbal when accompanying accents disrupts time the most, so use it wisely.
  • Always listen to the melody to decide how best to play the accents.
  • Always listen to the timbre of the horn players to decide how best to play the accents (trombone accents often work well with base drum, trumpet accents tend to sound nice on snare drum).

When played loudly:

  • Play fast accents (especially in fast songs) on snare drum and crash/ride cymbal.
  • Play shorter accents on snare drum and crash/ride cymbal or snare and (half-open) hi-hat.
  • Play longer accents on base drum and crash/ride cymbal.

When played softly or at medium volumes:

  • Play short accents on snare and longer accents with your base drum, but now without the use of any cymbals (except your ride cymbal for time-keeping).
  • Listenening to the timbre of the horns is extra important at soft volumes.
  • Keep the time on your ride going steady, since soft passages rarely call for emphasized accents.

Fills

With fills it's less clearcut, because if there is one domain where a drummer can show their own style, it's in their fills. This means that for the most part, you should just find your own style in playing fills! To help you along, here are a few tips and general conventions in playing fills:

  • It works well to have a contrasting sound between the fill and the accent. If you plan on playing an accent on snare drum, it sounds good to finish the fill on a low sounding drum like the base drum or floor tom (and vice versa).
  • Fills can be used to dictate the timing/feel of (strings of) accents, so experiment with playing fills in a way that will already suggest the feel of the notes to come.
  • In some scenario's it can also work well to play a contrasting rhythm (to the rhythm of the accent), but only do this when you are absolutely certain it won't confuse the horn players.

For both accents and fills, the number one tip and rule you should always follow: listen to the greats! A lot of playing done by the most well known big band drummers is great not because of the technical skill, but because they know when to play, what to play and why they play it!
